What is and is not a dinosaur?

A common misconception of the public is that everything shown in Jurrasic Park or The Land Before Time was a dinosaur. It's an easy mistake to make. Big old creature from dinosaur time? === Dinosaur. However, that just isn't true. Take for example, pterosaurs (i.e. pterodactyl). They are not in fact dinosaurs as they do not share the same common dinosaur ancestor. Additionally, many seas creatures were not dinosaurs either, including, Dimitrodon (the giant sail creature), Mosasaur (the giant whale like creature fromm the sea show in Jurrasic World) and Megalodon (the giant shark creature).

FUN FACT! Did you know that Sharks are older than Dinosaurs? Not only are sharks older than dinosaurs- they are older than trees!

(Dinosaurs first appeared some 230 million years ago, trees 380 million years ago, and the first sharks appeared some 450 million years ago.)

Velociraptors

Speaking of Jurrasic Park, for many impressionable youth, Jurrasic Park is their first introduction into the (totally rad) world of dinosaurs. This has led to many spin-off francises and discussions about the original film and it's accuracies and inaccuracies. When it was first made, Jurrasic Park was actually very true to what we knew about dinosaurs at the time (save for some creative liberties, like the frilled, venom-spitting Dilophosaurus who we then knew did not have such features). One of the inaccuracies that people love to point out is the wild difference between the Velociraptors in Jurrasic Park and what we know about Velociraptor today, which are closer in size to a turkey. You can see in the infographic below the comical difference in size and appearance.

While many casual people may have heard this, what Paleonerds know is that this isn't the full story! At the time of the first movie (1993), paleontologists didn't have as many fossils of "raptors" as we do today. The fossils which they called "velociraptor" were actually a group of fossils at the time of many different kinds of raptor theropods (carnivorous dinosaurs), including deinonychus, an 11 foot long predator whose name literally translates to "terrible claw". While the velociraptors in Jurrasic Park are a bit larger than deinonychus, it is most likely that they were based off of deinonychus rather than what we today call "velociraptor" (Mr. Turkey). That said, they are all awesome predators who do indeed hunt in packs and are very intelligent creatures. My personal favorite raptor is Utahraptor. What's yours?

Spinosaurus

Now, I may not personally be a huge spinosaurs fan, but if you plan on becoming a Paleonerd, then you HAVE to know about Spinosaurus, the running joke in the paleotology community.

All dinosaurs have changed drastically since the first scientific records of them dating back to the late 1800s. It's amazing to see how many our understanding of dinosaurs has changed over time. A lot of advances in knowledge have been made even since the 90's. However, while scientists are pretty darn confident we know what, for example, T-rex looks like now, one dinosaurs continues to evade and frustrate the ever living daylights out of all scientists and paleolovers: Spinosaurus.

I hesitated to even provide a picture because, as the joke goes, spinosaurus will probably look different next week. I am not even joking when I say one month scientists could say "O.K., so we beleive that this dinosaur was fully acquatic and walked on two feet," and the next say, "Actually, we believe this dinosaur was only semi-acquatic and it would have been impossible physically for it to only walk on twos, so it must have walked on fours." Here are just a few of it's reconstructions, most of which are from just the past couple decades!

The problem with Spinosaurus is we have very limited fossils for them from each site- most of them just fragments of the whole, like a single rib or only the back feet. What we do know, is it's the only theropod we have found that would have spent most of it's time in water (like a crocodile) consisting of a diet almost entirely of fish. Combined with the huge sail and unusual snout, it's not hard to see why some people find it their favorite. Maybe one day a complete fossil will be found, but until then, we just have to be content with the ever changing styles and Paleoart for this awesome dinosaur.

Tyrannosaurus Rex

Meet Sue, one of the best and most recent full scale replicas of a Trex. Ain't she a beauty? She is currently on display at the Field Museum of Natural History in Chicago. What makes her special is she is one of the largest, most extensive, and best preserved Tyrannosaurus rex fossils ever found, at over 90 percent recovered. We actually know a LOT about Trex, from their incredible eyesight (which Jurassic Park certainly got wrong), that their young were likely at least partially feathered, and that their vocalizations probably sounded less like tiger roars and more like gigantic bellowing hissing crocodiles. Click HERE to listen to what Trex likely sounded like based on the structures of their fossilized trechea and ancestry.

Of course, we have also learned a lot about many of other species of dinosaur too. Trex was one of the first theropod fossils ever categorically discovered and was thusly named "King of the Lizards", but that name is incorrect for several reasons. For example, we now know that dinosaurs were not exactly reptiles nor lizards (though they share a common ancestor), but an entirely different type of animal. And second, we now know of many other theropods that are much bigger and stronger than Trex. For example, the afforementioned Spinosaurus is actually bigger than Trex in both height and length. As for stronger, Allosaurus, while not bigger than Trex, reportedly was much stronger and was able to take down pray twice the size of Trex. As a Paleonerd, it's important to know this as claiming Trex to be the "strongest" or "biggest" carnivore will likely set of a chorus of other nerds echoing "Actually! 🤓". All that said, while Trex may not be the biggest, one thing most of us Paleonerds agree on is Trex is still the **BEST**. With a stronger bite force than any animal that has ever lived and a legend and icon of dinosauria ever since it's discovery in 1905, for many people around the world, Trex holds a special place in their hearts, and that is why Trex is the best dinosaur to ever walk the earth.

Chickens and Dinosaurs

You may have snidely heard from somewhere that chickens are the ancestors of the mighty trex. To be clear, this just isn't true. Chickens did not evolve from Trex. And birds did not evolve from dinosaurs... birds ARE dinosaurs.

Similar in the way that cats did not evolve from tigers but instead evolved from some common feline ancestor over 6 million years ago, birds and dinosaurs like Trex and Tricerotops, all evolved from the same dinosaur common ancestor. In other words, birds didn't evolve from some trex, but rather, bird ancestors lived alongside other theropods during the Triassic period. It's sad sometimes to think about the fact we will never get to see those amazing animals in person- you will never meet a tricerotops, a stegosaurus, or a trex (though, maybe that last one is for the best...). But when you truly observe birds, you can see it in the way that they walk and the shape of their feet, that they clearly are dinosaurs walking among us.

And Congratulations! Whether you like it or not, you are now a Paleonerd.
